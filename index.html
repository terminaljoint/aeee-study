Statistics & Mathematical Reasoning
2–3 QsNEW▼
Statistics
Mean = Σfx / ΣfFor grouped data; x = midpoint, f = frequency
Variance σ² = Σfx²/Σf − (x̄)²Easier computational form
Std deviation σ = √(Variance)AEEE usually asks for variance or SD directly
Median: middle value (sort data first)For grouped: L + [(n/2 − cf)/f] × h
Mode: most frequent valueFor grouped: L + [f₁−f₀ / 2f₁−f₀−f₂] × h
Mathematical reasoning (logic)

Contrapositive of "If P then Q" is "If not Q then not P" — same truth value as original

Converse: "If Q then P". Inverse: "If not P then not Q". Neither is equivalent to original.

p ∧ q (AND): true only when BOTH true. p ∨ q (OR): false only when BOTH false.

Negation of "all X are Y" → "some X are not Y". Negation of "some X are Y" → "no X are Y".

Reasoning questions are pure logic — no calculation. Identify statement type, apply contrapositive or negation. Easy 3 marks if you know the rules.

Semiconductors, Logic Gates & Communication
2–3 QsNEW▼
Semiconductor basics

Intrinsic semiconductor: pure Ge/Si — electrons = holes at any temperature

N-type: doped with pentavalent (P, As) — excess electrons (majority carriers)

P-type: doped with trivalent (B, Al) — excess holes (majority carriers)

Forward bias: p-side to +ve terminal; current flows; barrier decreases

Reverse bias: p-side to −ve terminal; only tiny leakage current; barrier increases

Logic gates (memorize all 5)
AND: Y = A·BOutput 1 only when BOTH inputs are 1
OR: Y = A+BOutput 1 when AT LEAST ONE input is 1
NOT: Y = ĀOutput is opposite of input
NAND: Y = (A·B)̄ = Ā+B̄AND + NOT; universal gate — can make any other gate
NOR: Y = (A+B)̄ = Ā·B̄OR + NOT; also universal gate
Communication systems
Modulation index μ = Am/AcAm = message amplitude, Ac = carrier; μ ≤ 1 for no distortion
Bandwidth of AM = 2fmfm = highest message frequency
Range: d = √(2Rh)Max line-of-sight; R = Earth radius, h = antenna height
Draw the truth table for each gate on paper once — you'll remember permanently. Logic gates appear every single year in AEEE.

Organic Chemistry — Named Reactions & Tests
3–4 Qs▼
Must-know named reactions

Aldol condensation — two aldehydes/ketones join to form β-hydroxy carbonyl compound

Reagent: dilute NaOH

Cannizzaro reaction — disproportionation of aldehyde with no α-H; gives acid + alcohol

Reagent: conc. NaOH; e.g. HCHO → CH₃OH + HCOONa

Grignard reagent (RMgX) — reacts with carbonyl to add R group; produces alcohol after hydrolysis

Solvent: dry ether

SN1 — unimolecular; 2-step; tertiary carbons, polar protic solvents; racemization

SN2 — bimolecular; 1-step; primary carbons, polar aprotic solvents; inversion of configuration

Markovnikov's rule — H adds to C with more H atoms already (electrophilic addition)

Hofmann bromamide degradation — RCONH₂ + Br₂ + NaOH → RNH₂ (amine with one fewer C)

Functional group tests

Aldehyde: gives silver mirror test (Tollen's), Fehling's test (brick red ppt)

Carboxylic acid: effervescence with Na₂CO₃ (CO₂ gas)

Alcohol: Lucas test — tertiary reacts immediately, primary doesn't

Don't memorize mechanisms step-by-step. Know: what goes in + conditions → what comes out. That's all AEEE tests.

Periodic Table — Trends & p-Block Elements
3–4 QsNEW▼
Periodic trends (direction matters!)
Atomic radius: ↑ down group, ← across periodDecreases left→right (more protons); increases top→bottom
Ionization energy: ↓ down group, → across periodOpposite of radius. Exceptions: Be>B, N>O (half-filled stability)
Electronegativity order: F>O>N>Cl>Br>C>HIncreases right and up the periodic table. Memorize this order.
Electron affinity: Cl > F (exception)F is smaller but electron repulsion makes Cl have higher EA
p-block key facts

Group 15 (N, P): NH₃ is basic, pyramidal sp³. N₂ very stable (triple bond).

Group 16 (O, S): H₂O is bent sp³ (2 lone pairs). H₂S has lower bp than H₂O despite higher MW.

Group 17 (Halogens): Cl₂ bleaches in presence of water (HOCl). Bond energy: Cl-Cl > F-F > Br-Br.

Group 18 (Noble gases): First noble gas compound XeF₂. He in balloons; Ar in light bulbs; Ne in neon signs.

AEEE tests "which is largest/most electronegative" — know direction of each trend and the two key exceptions: Be>B and N>O for ionization energy.
Coordination Compounds
2–3 QsNEW▼
Nomenclature rules

Name order: ligands (alphabetical) → central metal → oxidation state in Roman numerals → counter ion

Example: [Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]Cl = tetraamminedichloridocobalt(III) chloride

Anionic ligands end in "-o": Cl⁻ = chlorido, CN⁻ = cyanido, OH⁻ = hydroxido, NO₂⁻ = nitrito

Neutral ligands: NH₃ = ammine, H₂O = aqua, CO = carbonyl, NO = nitrosyl

Coordination number = ligands attached to metalCN=6 → octahedral; CN=4 → tetrahedral or square planar

Oxidation state calculation: metal OS + ligand charges + counter ion charges = 0

Example: [Fe(CN)₆]³⁻ → Fe + 6(−1) = −3, so Fe = +3

Isomerism: Geometric (cis-trans) for MA₂B₂ octahedral. Optical (non-superimposable mirror images).

Finding oxidation state appears every year — practice with 5 examples: [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺, [Fe(CN)₆]³⁻, [Cr(H₂O)₆]³⁺, [Co(en)₃]³⁺, [Ni(CO)₄].

BCC: 2 atoms/cell, packing 68%1 (corners) + 1 (centre) = 2. Na, K, Fe.
FCC: 4 atoms/cell, packing 74%1 (corners) + 3 (faces) = 4. Cu, Al, Au — densest packing.
d = nM / (Nₐ × a³)Density; n = atoms/cell, a = edge length
Ionic equilibrium
Ka = [H⁺][A⁻] / [HA]Acid dissociation constant; stronger acid → larger Ka
pH = −log[H⁺]pH + pOH = 14 at 25°C. Neutral water: pH=7
pH of weak acid = ½(pKa − log C)C = initial concentration of acid
Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])Buffer equation — salt/acid buffer solutions
Ksp = [Mⁿ⁺][Xⁿ⁻] (for MX salt)Precipitation when Q > Ksp
Buffer and Ksp questions appear every year. For MₐXᵦ salt → Ksp = (as)ᵃ × (bs)ᵇ where s = solubility.
Surface Chemistry & Colloids
2 QsNEW▼

Physisorption: weak van der Waals, reversible, exothermic, decreases with temperature, multilayer

Chemisorption: chemical bond, irreversible, forms monolayer, initially increases then decreases with temp

Colloid size: 1–1000 nm (between true solution <1 nm and suspension >1000 nm)

Tyndall effect: scattering of light by colloidal particles — distinguishes colloid from true solution

Hardy-Schulze rule: higher valency of coagulating ion = more effective coagulation of colloid

Activation energy with catalyst: E_a' < E_aCatalyst lowers activation energy; doesn't affect equilibrium position
Surface chemistry MCQs are mostly conceptual. Know: Tyndall effect, physisorption vs chemisorption comparison, Hardy-Schulze rule for coagulation.

Important polymers

Biomolecules

Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆): reducing sugar; open chain has aldehyde; gives Tollen's and Fehling's test

Sucrose: glucose + fructose; NON-reducing (no free anomeric OH). Fructose = sweetest sugar.

Amino acids: contain both −NH₂ and −COOH groups. Peptide bond = −CO−NH− linkage.

DNA: thymine, deoxyribose sugar, double helix. RNA: uracil, ribose sugar, single stranded.

Thermoplastic = can be reshaped (Nylon, PVC, Teflon). Thermosetting = cannot be reshaped (Bakelite). Addition polymer = one monomer. Condensation = two monomers + small molecule released.
d-Block (Transition) Elements
2 QsNEW▼

d-block: Groups 3–12 (Sc to Zn). Variable oxidation states due to similar energies of ns and (n−1)d electrons.

Most stable states: Mn²⁺ (d⁵ half-filled), Fe³⁺ (d⁵), Cu²⁺ more stable than Cu⁺ in aqueous solution

Paramagnetism: due to unpaired d electrons. More unpaired = more paramagnetic.

Catalysts: Fe in Haber (N₂+3H₂→2NH₃); V₂O₅ in Contact process; Ni in hydrogenation; Pt in catalytic converters

Colour in solution: due to d-d electron transitions. Cu²⁺=blue, Cr³⁺=green, Mn²⁺=pale pink, Fe³⁺=yellow

KMnO₄: MnO₄⁻ → Mn²⁺ (gains 5e⁻, purple→colourless)Strong oxidizing agent in acid medium. Oxidizes Fe²⁺, I⁻, oxalates.
K₂Cr₂O₇: Cr₂O₇²⁻ → 2Cr³⁺ (gains 6e⁻, orange→green)Oxidizes alcohols to aldehydes/acids in acid medium.
Know the catalyst-process pairs, common solution colours of ions, and both oxidizing agents (KMnO₄ and K₂Cr₂O₇) — these are 90% conceptual marks.

Simple & Compound Interest, Number Series
3 Qs▼
SI = PRT / 100P = principal, R = rate%, T = time in years
CI: A = P(1 + R/100)ᵀAmount after T years; CI = A − P
CI − SI = P(R/100)² for 2 yrsQuick shortcut for 2-year difference questions
Number series patterns

Arithmetic series: each term differs by constant d (find d = T₂ − T₁)

Geometric series: each term multiplied by constant r (find r = T₂/T₁)

Difference series: check differences of consecutive terms — often form AP/GP themselves

Square/cube series: 1,4,9,16... or 1,8,27,64... — spot perfect squares or cubes

Strategy: For number series, immediately compute differences between consecutive terms. Constant → AP. Constant ratio → GP. Differences form AP → quadratic sequence.

Grammar — Articles, Prepositions, Tenses & Verbs
2 Qs▼
Articles (a, an, the)

"a" → before consonant sounds: a book, a university (note: 'u' sounds like 'y')

"an" → before vowel sounds: an apple, an hour (note: 'h' is silent here)

"the" → specific/known noun; unique things (the sun); superlatives (the best)

No article → general plural nouns (Dogs are loyal), languages, subjects (I study Physics)

Prepositions
in: months, years, citiesin January / in 2026 / in Chennai
on: days, dates, surfaceson Monday / on 21st April / on the table
at: specific time or placeat 9 AM / at the station / at home
Common tense rules

Simple past: use "did" in question/negative; no "-ed" with "did" (I did go, not I did went)

Present perfect: have/has + past participle — for actions with present relevance (I have studied)

Subject-verb agreement: singular subject → singular verb (The team is, not The team are)
